About

Automatic differentiation is increasingly being adopted beyond machine learning to tackle optimization problems in various scientific and engineering contexts. These methods have enabled the development of differentiable components such as simulators, mathematical solvers, surrogate models, 3D renderers, and more, under the umbrella of differentiable programming and scientific machine learning (SciML). Despite early successes of such components and simple applications based on them (that often outperform their non-differentiable or pure-ML counterparts), the community has yet to move beyond the stage of isolated prototypes using one or two components towards end-to-end differentiable systems. This gives rise to practical difficulties, for example when it comes to propagating gradients through discrete operations, ensuring stability and usefulness of gradients, or integrating heterogeneous pieces of software, and conceptual challenges, for example regarding the high-level design and real-world usefulness of differentiable systems.

This workshop brings together experts from around the community to:

  • Assess relevance of system-level differentiability to the SciML practitioners
  • Inform future directions of end-to-end differentiable systems with real-world impact
  • Formulate a near-term research and engineering agenda to establish differentiable systems as an effective tool for the community

Call for Papers

We invite submissions in a form of short papers, including those describing work in progress, on the following topics:

  • Differentiable models for scientific ML
  • Multi-scale and multi-physics differentiable modeling
  • Theory and practice of automatic differentiation
  • Challenges of moving from differentiable models to end-to-end differentiable systems
  • Design and implementation of differentiable systems
  • Gradient tracing across distributed computing environments
  • Novel ways to arrive at differentiable (SciML) components
  • Concrete prototypes of differentiable systems
  • Theoretical insight into properties of differentiable systems
  • Integration of differentiable components with experimental workflows
  • Differentiable approaches to inverse problems and parameter estimation
  • Case studies across scientific domains: climate, physics, engineering design
  • Alternative methods of performing optimization (such as Bayesian optimization)

Submission Guidelines

  • Length: up to 4 pages plus unlimited references
  • Format: NeurIPS 2025 template
  • Reviews: double-blind, please anonymise your submission
  • Proceedings: no format proceedings, accepted papers will be shared on the website
